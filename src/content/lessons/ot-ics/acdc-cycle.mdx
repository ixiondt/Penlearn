## The cycle, not the moment

Most defense thinking is event-shaped: "an alert fired, what do we do?" Robert M.
Lee's **Active Cyber Defense Cycle (ACDC)** reorganizes the work as a continuous
five-step loop, each step feeding the next, with explicit handoff artifacts.
Step 5 closes back to Step 1, so what you learn from one intrusion becomes the
threat intelligence that drives the next iteration.

```text
1. Threat Intelligence    ──>  2. Asset Identification
   Consumption                       │
                                     ▼
5. Threat & Environment   <──  3. Network Security Monitoring
   Manipulation                      │
        ▲                            ▼
        └─────── 4. Incident Response ──┘
```

**ACDC is defensive only.** It does not mean "hack back" — every action lives
inside your own authority boundary. The premise is that the adversary is
already past the perimeter and that automated tooling missed them, so the
defender's job is to find them deliberately and turn the engagement into
intelligence that hardens the environment.

## The toolkit script

`acdc-cycle.sh` scaffolds an ACDC workspace, tracks state per step, and renders
an After-Action Report at the end:

```bash
# Stand up a new cycle for a hunt engagement
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh init --engagement acme-q2 --scope 'External hunt + OT pivot'

# Move through the steps as the engagement progresses
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh step1 --engagement acme-q2 --ti-feed external-ti.json
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh step2 --engagement acme-q2 --asset-list crown-jewels.csv
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh step3 --engagement acme-q2 --zeek-dir /var/log/zeek/
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh step4 --engagement acme-q2 --incident-id INC-2026-014
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh step5 --engagement acme-q2

# Check status any time
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh status --engagement acme-q2

# Render the AAR (markdown, ready to share)
./scripts/acdc-cycle.sh report --engagement acme-q2 --output acdc-aar.md
```

Each step lives in its own directory under `~/acdc/<engagement>/`, with a
seeded `README.md` that lists the required artifacts and the toolkit commands
that produce them.

## Step-by-step: what feeds what

**Step 1 — Threat Intelligence Consumption.** You don't "subscribe to feeds";
you pick what's relevant to your environment, classify it (Strategic /
Operational / Tactical), translate it into testable hunt hypotheses, and hand
those to Step 3.

**Step 2 — Asset Identification.** Know what you're defending. Crown jewels,
dependencies, baselines. The dependencies matter because containment in Step 4
cannot break the mission — you must know what a Crown Jewel depends on before
you can isolate anything safely.

**Step 3 — Network Security Monitoring.** Are the things from Step 1 visible
against the things from Step 2? Three disciplines: **collection** (am I
capturing what I'd need?), **detection** (will the analytics fire?), **analysis**
(can a human triage it?).

**Step 4 — Incident Response.** Detection trips, you investigate, contain,
eradicate, recover. For OT environments, containment is gated by the
three-stakeholder rule — IR Lead + NetO + Process Engineer must all sign off.
The Diamond Model artifact lives here.

**Step 5 — Threat & Environment Manipulation.** Within your authority, deny /
disrupt / degrade the adversary, and harden the environment so the same TTP
doesn't work twice. Populate the Course of Action matrix; push outbound CTI
to peers; feed lessons back into Step 1 for the next iteration.

## Closing the loop

You're done with one full ACDC iteration when you can answer three questions
that will improve next round's Step 1:

1. What new threat actor or TTP did we encounter?
2. What hunt hypothesis got validated or refuted?
3. What detection gap did we close?

If you can't answer all three, the cycle isn't done — the weakest step needs
another pass.

## ACDC vs. other frameworks

ACDC's value is integration: it gives you a structure for how to use the
others together with named handoffs.

| Framework | When you reach for it |
|-----------|------------------------|
| NIST SP 800-61 | Authoritative IR phase model — used **inside** ACDC's Step 4 |
| SANS Sliding Scale | Architecture → Passive → Active → Intelligence → Offence — ACDC sits in "Active" |
| ICS Cyber Kill Chain | Adversary-side model — ACDC is the defender-side cycle |
| Diamond Model | Single-intrusion analysis — fits **inside** Step 4's outputs |
| MITRE ATT&CK | Technique taxonomy — tag and pivot within every ACDC step |
