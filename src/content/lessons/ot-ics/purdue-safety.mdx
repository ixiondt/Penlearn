## The priority that overrides everything

OT/ICS work has a priority stack that defenders coming from IT must internalize before
touching anything:

```text
1. Human safety       — Always. No exceptions.
2. Process safety     — The physical process operating within design limits.
3. Mission            — The plant produces what it's supposed to.
4. Investigation      — Finding the attacker.
```

Investigation comes fourth. Not first, not third — fourth. The pressure during an IT
incident to "contain the threat" can kill people in an OT context. A firewall ACL that
isolates a "compromised" RTU can take down process safety. A "harmless" port scan can
crash a 20-year-old PLC. The defender's discipline is to recognize that the attacker
caused the incident, but a clumsy responder can cause the casualties.

## The Purdue model in one diagram

```text
Level 5 — Enterprise / Corporate IT      (your normal email, file servers, etc.)
─────────────────────────────────────── DMZ ────────────────────────────────────
Level 4 — Site Business Planning         (ERP, MES — still "IT-shaped")
─────────────────────────────────── IDMZ (firewall) ────────────────────────────
Level 3 — Operations Management          (historian, engineering workstations)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Level 2 — Supervisory Control            (HMIs, SCADA servers)
Level 1 — Basic Control                  (PLCs, RTUs, DCS controllers)
Level 0 — Physical Process               (sensors, actuators, valves, motors)
```

Levels 0-3 are the OT side. Level 3.5 (the IDMZ) is the only safe traversal point.
Levels 0-1 are physically wired to the plant — a misbehavior there has physical
consequences in milliseconds.

Defenders coming from IT often want to treat the OT network as a flat zone. It isn't.
Each level has different containment rules, different acquisition rules, and different
authorization gates.

## The three-stakeholder gate

Any OT containment action requires three approvals — not as a process formality, but
because no one stakeholder has the knowledge to make the call safely:

1. **IR Lead** — owns incident response decisions
2. **Network Operations** — knows the network topology, what depends on what
3. **Process Engineer** — knows whether the proposed isolation is physically safe

The toolkit's `scripts/ics-containment.sh` enforces this. `--execute` is gated behind
explicit confirmation from all three roles. Generate-only mode (the default) is for
drafting the proposed action and circulating it for review.

```bash
# Draft the action — generates a script with the firewall commands, doesn't run them
./scripts/ics-containment.sh zone-isolate --zone 3 --firewall-ip 10.0.0.1

# Run it — requires IR Lead + NetO + Process Engineer confirmations interactively
./scripts/ics-containment.sh zone-isolate --zone 3 --firewall-ip 10.0.0.1 --execute
```

The gate is friction by design. The gate is the safety mechanism.

## Why active scanning is forbidden in OT

A standard nmap scan against a PLC can:

- Crash the PLC entirely (old firmware, fragile TCP stack)
- Send the PLC into a fault state requiring physical reset
- Trigger watchdog timers that take a plant offline
- Force a hot standby failover that causes a brief loss of control

Nmap's `-sV` and `-O` flags are particularly dangerous in OT. Any service detection
that sends unusual protocol traffic can confuse a PLC. The version of `nmap` doesn't
matter — the PLC is what's fragile.

The defensive equivalent is **passive analysis**. Capture a PCAP, analyze it with
Zeek's ICS analyzers and tshark's OT dissectors. The toolkit's
`scripts/ics-protocol-analysis.sh` does exactly this — Modbus, DNP3, EtherNet/IP,
OPC UA, S7comm, BACnet, IEC-104 — without ever sending a packet.

## Evidence acquisition in OT

The volatile-first order from IT still applies, but with safety overrides:

- **Memory acquisition on an engineering workstation** — usually fine. Workstations
  are Windows or Linux, not protocol-specific.
- **Memory acquisition on an HMI** — proceed with caution. HMIs are often tightly
  coupled to a SCADA server. The acquisition tool itself may interfere.
- **Memory acquisition on a PLC** — almost never. The PLC's vendor-specific tooling
  is the right path, and only with vendor support engaged.
- **PCAP capture** — almost always safe and the highest-value OT artifact. Span port
  or network tap. The toolkit's `scripts/ics-forensics-collect.sh` walks this by zone.

If you can't safely acquire evidence at a particular level, that's an acquisition
gap to document — not a license to skip directly to containment.

## The standing rules that map to OT

From CLAUDE.md §8:

> 6. OT/ICS isolation requires NetO alignment and business authority approval. Process
>    engineers confirm physical safety. All three stakeholders required — this can be
>    one coordinated step but cannot proceed without all three. No active scanning in
>    OT networks.

> 10. Rebuild when in doubt. Manual cleanup has a higher miss rate than a clean rebuild.
>     Uncertainty about full attacker access resolves toward rebuild.

The "rebuild when in doubt" rule lands differently in OT. A controller in a production
line isn't a laptop you can swap. Rebuild may mean staged restoration during a planned
downtime window. The IR plan has to account for this — you may operate in a degraded
state for weeks while restoration is scheduled.
