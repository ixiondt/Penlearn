## Containment that could hurt someone

In IT, isolating a compromised host is almost always the safe call. In OT,
isolating the wrong device mid-process can be *more* dangerous than the intrusion
— cut a controller off from its safety logic or its operator at the wrong moment
and you can cause the physical incident you were trying to prevent. OT containment
is therefore gated by people, not just by review. `ics-containment.sh` enforces
that.

## Safety-first priority order

Every OT decision is ranked against this hierarchy — and it never changes:

```text
Human safety  >  Process safety  >  Mission  >  Investigation
```

Catching the attacker (investigation) is the **lowest** priority. If a containment
action might endanger people or the safe state of the process, you don't take it —
even if that means the attacker stays connected a while longer. This is the
opposite of the IT instinct to isolate immediately.

## Generate-only, with a three-stakeholder gate

```bash
# Generates the firewall ACL / block commands for review — never auto-executes
./scripts/ics-containment.sh zone-isolate --zone 3 --firewall-ip 10.0.0.1
./scripts/ics-containment.sh block-protocol --protocol modbus --src 10.1.0.5 --dst 10.2.0.10
```

Like the IT `containment.sh`, it defaults to generate-only. But execution requires
**three independent stakeholders to agree** — this can be one coordinated step,
but it cannot proceed without all three:

```text
IR Lead            ── the security decision
Network Operations ── confirms the network change won't break OT comms
Process Engineer   ── confirms physical safety of isolating that zone/device
```

No single person — not even the IR lead — can authorize OT containment alone.
The process engineer's sign-off is the one IT incidents don't have: *will cutting
this off leave the plant in a safe state?*

## Purdue-zone-aware isolation

Containment operates on the Purdue model's network zones, not individual hosts:

```text
zone-isolate     firewall-block a Purdue zone (e.g. isolate L3 from L2)
block-protocol   block a specific OT protocol between two endpoints
emergency-checklist   the pre-agreed steps for a given zone
```

You isolate at the IT/OT boundary or between zones where it's safe to do so —
keeping control and safety systems intact — rather than yanking a field device.

## Why this gate exists

```text
IT containment:   isolate host now ─▶ worst case: a service is down
OT containment:   isolate device now ─▶ worst case: unsafe process state, injury
                                            │
                                   therefore: 3-stakeholder gate, safety > investigation
```

The three-stakeholder gate isn't bureaucracy — it's the recognition that an OT
containment error has physical consequences a security person isn't equipped to
judge alone. The toolkit encodes this as a hard gate (`three_stakeholder_gate()`)
so the safest path is the default path. When in doubt in OT, you slow down and get
the right people in the room before you act.
