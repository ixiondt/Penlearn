## Why authorization is the first lesson

Every other lesson on this site is downstream of one rule: **active testing requires written authorization**.
The reason isn't bureaucratic. It's that the difference between "security professional" and "unauthorized
intruder" — legally, ethically, and operationally — is the document on your desk before you press enter.

The SecOps toolkit codifies this as its first standing rule:

> Authorization before action. Written scope or engagement letter required before any active testing.
> Passive recon against public data does not require authorization.

Internalize the split between **passive** and **active** before doing anything else. Get it wrong and a
"quick nmap to see what's there" becomes a criminal offense in most jurisdictions.

## Passive vs. active — the operational definition

| Category | Touches target | Examples | Authorization required |
|----------|---------------|----------|------------------------|
| Passive  | No            | WHOIS, certificate transparency logs, public DNS resolvers querying you, public breach data, Shodan/Censys results that already exist, OSINT framework queries | No |
| Active   | Yes           | Port scans, banner grabs, web fuzzing, vulnerability scans, credential spraying, social engineering, anything that creates a log entry on the target | **Yes — written** |

A useful test: *would the target's logs show I was there?* If yes, that's active.

The boundary line is sharper than people think. Running `dig` against a public resolver to look up
`example.com` is passive. Running `dig +trace` so your recursor asks `example.com`'s authoritative
nameservers directly creates packets at the target. The first is fine. The second is active.

## What "written authorization" actually means

You need one of these before the active engagement starts:

- **Scope document** — for retained pentest work, signed by someone who can authorize testing on the target's behalf
- **Engagement letter** — for one-off assessments
- **Rules of engagement (ROE)** — for red team / purple team / longer engagements; usually includes communication channels, abort criteria, blackout windows
- **Internal authorization** — for in-house security teams, a documented program with explicit management sign-off

The document should answer at minimum:

1. **What** — IP ranges, domains, applications, user accounts, physical locations
2. **What's excluded** — production-critical systems, third-party SaaS, customers' infrastructure
3. **When** — start, end, blackout windows
4. **How aggressive** — denial-of-service permitted? destructive testing? social engineering?
5. **Who's the contact** — names, phone numbers, escalation path, a 24/7 number
6. **Who signed it** — the person authorizing must have actual authority

If any of those are missing or vague, ask. Don't proceed on assumption.

## The standing rule in the toolkit

The toolkit's CLAUDE.md §8 makes this explicit:

> Authorization before action. Written scope or engagement letter required before any active testing.
> Passive recon against public data does not require authorization.

And §1 of the standing rules:

> When uncertain, treat as the higher severity level.

When in doubt about whether something is "active enough" to need authorization, treat it as active.
The cost of a confirmation email is zero. The cost of being wrong is your career.

## Common authorization gotchas

- **Third-party services.** The scope says "test our app." The app uses Stripe, Cloudflare, and Auth0.
  You don't have authorization for those — and Stripe in particular has aggressive lawyers. Your scope
  needs to either exclude them or have the operator confirm out-of-band that hitting them is fine.
- **Cloud assets.** AWS, Azure, and GCP all have published pentest policies. AWS dropped the pre-auth
  requirement in 2019 for most services, but **denial of service** and **simulated phishing** still
  require explicit AWS approval. Read the cloud provider's policy.
- **Shared infrastructure.** A VPS at DigitalOcean? The customer owns the VM, but the hypervisor and
  network are DO's. Read their AUP. Most cloud providers permit testing of your own resources but
  forbid testing that affects neighbors.
- **Production.** "Test the app" usually means staging or pre-prod. Confirm explicitly before pointing
  any active tool at a production system.
- **DNS providers.** Running aggressive subdomain brute-forcing through a CDN provider's resolvers can
  trigger that provider's anti-abuse. You may be authorized to test the customer; you're not authorized
  to abuse Cloudflare's 1.1.1.1.

## Why this matters for the rest of the curriculum

Every active-mode module in this site has the same prerequisite: you have a target you're allowed to
test. The labs handle this by being self-hosted — the targets are vulnerable VMs you stand up on your
own machine, so the authorization is implicit. The toolkit, however, will happily point at any IP you
give it. The discipline is yours.

The toolkit even bakes in a confirmation gate in `scripts/lib/common.sh` (`confirm_action`) that
several active scripts use. It's not a replacement for written authorization — it's a tripwire to
make sure you've paused.

## Action items before you advance

1. Read CLAUDE.md §8 of your SecOps toolkit clone — the full standing rules, not just rule #1.
2. Bookmark the cloud provider pentest policies for the providers you work with most.
3. Create a `00-authorization.txt` template in your toolkit clone you can drop into every engagement.
4. Decide your personal rule for "uncertain" cases. The toolkit's default is "treat as higher severity" —
   yours should be at least that strict.
